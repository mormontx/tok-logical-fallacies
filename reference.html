Logical Fallacies Reference Guide

Understanding and Identifying Common Reasoning Errors

üìö What Are Logical Fallacies?

Logical fallacies are errors in reasoning that undermine the logic of an argument. They often seem persuasive and can be emotionally compelling, but they don't hold up under logical scrutiny. Understanding fallacies helps you evaluate arguments critically and construct stronger arguments yourself.

  • Questions to Ask: Critical thinking prompts
1. Ad Hominem
Also known as: "Attack the Person," "Personal Attack"
Relevance Fallacy
üìñ Definition
Attacking the person making an argument rather than addressing the argument itself. The speaker dismisses or rejects someone's position by attacking their character, circumstances, or personal attributes instead of engaging with the substance of their claim.
üîç How to Spot It:
  • Focus shifts from the argument to the person making it
  • Phrases like "You can't trust them because..." or "They're just..."
  • Bringing up irrelevant personal details (age, appearance, past mistakes)
  • Questioning motives instead of addressing evidence
  • Character assassination or name-calling
üìù Example:
"Sarah argues we should change the dress code, but she was late to school twice last month, so we can't trust her judgment on school policies."
‚ùå Why It's Fallacious:

A person's character flaws, mistakes, or personal attributes don't automatically make their arguments wrong. Even unreliable people can make valid points. The truth of a claim should be evaluated based on evidence and logic, not on who's making the claim.

‚ùì Questions to Ask:
  • Is the criticism relevant to the argument being made?
  • Would the argument be different if someone else made it?
  • Are they addressing what was said or who said it?
  • Is there any actual evidence presented against the claim?
2. Appeal to Inappropriate Authority
Also known as: "False Authority," "Argument from Authority"
Relevance Fallacy
üìñ Definition
Using the opinion of an expert or authority figure who is NOT actually an expert in the relevant field, or implying that expertise in one area automatically transfers to all areas.
üîç How to Spot It:
  • "Dr. X says..." when Dr. X isn't an expert in that specific field
  • Celebrity endorsements as evidence
  • "This successful person believes..." (success ‚â† expertise in all areas)
  • Credentials in an unrelated field being used as proof
  • Vague references to "experts" or "studies" without specific qualifications
üìù Example:
"Dr. Smith, a renowned cardiologist, says climate change is exaggerated. He went to Harvard, so we should trust his opinion on climate science."
‚ùå Why It's Fallacious:

Expertise in one field doesn't automatically transfer to another. A cardiologist's medical training doesn't make them an expert in climate science. Being intelligent, educated, or successful doesn't mean someone is knowledgeable about every topic.

‚ùì Questions to Ask:
  • Is this person actually an expert in THIS specific field?
  • What are their qualifications in this area?
  • Would a legitimate expert in this field agree?
  • Is there a consensus among actual experts?
3. Appeal to Tradition
Also known as: "We've Always Done It This Way"
Relevance Fallacy
üìñ Definition
Arguing that something is correct or better simply because it's traditional, old, or "the way we've always done it." The argument assumes that longevity equals correctness.
üîç How to Spot It:
  • "We've always done it this way"
  • "This worked for our ancestors/founders/previous generations"
  • "This is a time-honored tradition"
  • "If it's not broken, why fix it?"
  • Emphasizing how long something has existed as proof of its value
üìù Example:
"Our school has had a uniform policy for 50 years. We should never change it because it's a tradition that has served us well."
‚ùå Why It's Fallacious:

Age doesn't determine correctness. Many traditional practices were based on outdated knowledge or different circumstances. Just because something has been done for a long time doesn't mean it's optimal, ethical, or still relevant. Circumstances change, knowledge advances.

‚ùì Questions to Ask:
  • Why was this tradition started in the first place?
  • Have circumstances changed since then?
  • Is there actual evidence it's still beneficial?
  • Are we keeping it because it works or just because it's familiar?
4. Appeal to Popularity (Bandwagon)
Also known as: "Argumentum ad Populum," "Everyone's Doing It"
Relevance Fallacy
üìñ Definition
Arguing that something must be true or good because many people believe it or do it. Using popularity as evidence of correctness.
üîç How to Spot It:
  • "Everyone knows that..."
  • "Most people agree..."
  • "All the successful/smart/educated people believe..."
  • "The majority can't be wrong"
  • Using polling data or consensus as sole evidence
  • Peer pressure framed as argument
üìù Example:
"Every responsible parent I know agrees that social media is harmful. All eight parents at my book club nodded in agreement. That's consensus."
‚ùå Why It's Fallacious:

Popular belief doesn't equal truth. History is full of examples where the majority was wrong (flat earth, geocentrism, etc.). Additionally, the sample might not be representative, and what's popular can be influenced by many factors unrelated to truth or quality.

‚ùì Questions to Ask:
  • Is this a representative sample or just a specific group?
  • What evidence do these people have for their belief?
  • Have popular beliefs been wrong before?
  • What would change if the consensus shifted?
5. Anecdotal Evidence
Also known as: "Personal Experience Fallacy"
Weak Evidence Fallacy
üìñ Definition
Using personal stories, individual cases, or isolated examples as proof of a general claim, instead of systematic evidence or statistical data.
üîç How to Spot It:
  • "I know someone who..." or "My friend/relative..."
  • Single stories used to prove broad claims
  • "In my experience..."
  • "I once heard about..."
  • One or two examples presented as representative
  • "This happened to me, so it must be true for everyone"
üìù Example:
"My friend Jennifer switched to an organic diet and lost 10 pounds. This proves organic food causes weight loss."
‚ùå Why It's Fallacious:

Individual cases can be influenced by many factors and may not represent typical experiences. One person's experience doesn't establish patterns. There could be confounding variables, coincidence, or the person might be an outlier. Systematic data from many cases is needed for reliable conclusions.

‚ùì Questions to Ask:
  • How many cases are being presented?
  • Could other factors explain this person's experience?
  • Is this experience typical or exceptional?
  • What do systematic studies show?
6. False Cause (Post Hoc)
Also known as: "Correlation ‚â† Causation," "Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc"
Causal Fallacy
üìñ Definition
Assuming that because two things are correlated or one event followed another, one must have caused the other. Confusing correlation with causation.
üîç How to Spot It:
  • "After X happened, Y happened, so X caused Y"
  • "Studies show correlation, therefore causation"
  • Temporal sequence used as proof of causation
  • "Every time X occurs, Y follows"
  • Ignoring other possible explanations
  • Assuming direct causation from statistical association
üìù Example:
"Eastside High built a new stadium and won the championship the next year. This proves expensive facilities guarantee success."
‚ùå Why It's Fallacious:

Correlation doesn't prove causation. There could be: (1) reverse causation (B caused A, not A caused B), (2) a third variable causing both, (3) pure coincidence, or (4) a complex interaction of factors. Many things correlate without causing each other.

‚ùì Questions to Ask:
  • Could the causation go the other direction?
  • What other factors might explain both events?
  • Is there a plausible mechanism for causation?
  • Could this be coincidence?
7. False Dilemma (Either/Or)
Also known as: "Black-and-White Thinking," "False Dichotomy"
Presumption Fallacy
üìñ Definition
Presenting only two options as if they're the only possibilities, when in fact there are other alternatives. Forcing a choice between extremes while ignoring middle ground or other options.
üîç How to Spot It:
  • "Either we do X or Y happens" (presenting two extremes)
  • "You're either with us or against us"
  • "We have only two choices..."
  • "It's all or nothing"
  • Ignoring middle-ground positions
  • "If you don't support X, you must support Y"
üìù Example:
"Either we adopt iPads immediately and prepare for the modern world, or we continue with outdated textbooks and watch our students fail. There's no middle ground."
‚ùå Why It's Fallacious:

Most issues have multiple options, not just two extremes. There are usually compromise positions, alternative solutions, or gradual approaches. By limiting options artificially, the argument manipulates choice and ignores complexity.

‚ùì Questions to Ask:
  • Are there really only two options?
  • What middle-ground positions exist?
  • What other alternatives haven't been mentioned?
  • Could we combine elements of both options?
8. Slippery Slope
Also known as: "Domino Theory," "Thin End of the Wedge"
Causal Fallacy
üìñ Definition
Arguing that one small step will inevitably lead to a chain of events resulting in extreme consequences, without providing evidence for this chain reaction.
üîç How to Spot It:
  • "If we allow X, then Y will happen, then Z, and eventually disaster"
  • Long chain of hypothetical consequences
  • "This is the first step toward..."
  • "Give an inch, they'll take a mile"
  • Catastrophic predictions without evidence of inevitability
  • "Once we start down this path..."
üìù Example:
"If we allow ripped jeans, next students will want shorts, then tank tops, then swimsuits. Soon school will look like a beach party."
‚ùå Why It's Fallacious:

The argument assumes each step must inevitably lead to the next without explaining why or providing evidence. Moderate changes can stay moderate. The chain reaction isn't proven‚Äîit's assumed. Each step in the chain needs its own justification.

‚ùì Questions to Ask:
  • Is there evidence that each step leads to the next?
  • Can't we stop at any point in this chain?
  • Have similar policies led to these extreme outcomes elsewhere?
  • What prevents us from drawing a line somewhere in the middle?
9. Hasty Generalization
Also known as: "Jumping to Conclusions," "Insufficient Sample"
Weak Evidence Fallacy
üìñ Definition
Drawing a broad conclusion based on a small or unrepresentative sample. Making sweeping claims from insufficient evidence.
üîç How to Spot It:
  • Small sample size used to prove general rule
  • "All X are Y" based on limited examples
  • "This always happens" based on few cases
  • Generalizing from exceptional cases
  • Using one or two examples to prove a universal claim
üìù Example:
"I asked 20 colleagues if tests are stressful, and 18 said yes. Therefore, everyone finds tests stressful, so they must be harmful."
‚ùå Why It's Fallacious:

The sample is too small or unrepresentative to support the broad conclusion. You can't reliably generalize from a few cases to all cases. The examples might be exceptions rather than the rule, or the sample might be biased.

‚ùì Questions to Ask:
  • How many examples support this claim?
  • Is the sample representative of the whole group?
  • Could these be exceptions rather than the rule?
  • What percentage of cases does this actually represent?
10. Cherry Picking
Also known as: "Selective Evidence," "Suppressed Evidence"
Weak Evidence Fallacy
üìñ Definition
Selecting only favorable evidence while ignoring or dismissing contradictory evidence. Presenting a biased picture by highlighting data that supports your position and hiding what doesn't.
üîç How to Spot It:
  • Citing only studies that support their view
  • Ignoring contradictory data or research
  • Using a single exceptional case as representative
  • Dismissing counterevidence as "biased" or "unreliable"
  • Highlighting positive data while downplaying negative
üìù Example:
"It snowed heavily last week with eight inches of snow. If global warming were real, how could we have such intense snowfall?"
‚ùå Why It's Fallacious:

By selecting only favorable evidence, you create a misleading picture. One data point doesn't refute a trend. Local weather doesn't disprove global climate patterns. A full, honest evaluation requires considering all relevant evidence, not just what supports your pre-existing view.

‚ùì Questions to Ask:
  • What evidence is being left out?
  • Are contradictory findings being addressed?
  • Is this example representative or exceptional?
  • What does the broader body of evidence show?
11. Appeal to Uncertainty
Also known as: "Argument from Ignorance (variant)"
Presumption Fallacy
üìñ Definition
Arguing that because we're not 100% certain about something, we can't trust it at all. Using the inherent uncertainty in science or knowledge as a reason to dismiss well-supported conclusions.
üîç How to Spot It:
  • "Scientists keep changing their minds"
  • "If they're not 100% sure, we can't trust them"
  • "There's always contradictory research"
  • "Since experts disagree, we can't know anything"
  • Exploiting normal scientific revision as proof of unreliability
üìù Example:
"Scientists said eggs were bad, then good. Coffee was bad, then good. They keep changing their minds, so we can't trust nutritional science at all."
‚ùå Why It's Fallacious:

Science progresses by updating knowledge based on new evidence‚Äîthis is a strength, not a weakness. Perfect certainty is rarely achievable, but that doesn't mean all claims are equally uncertain. We can have high confidence in well-supported conclusions even if we don't have absolute certainty.

‚ùì Questions to Ask:
  • What's the current level of scientific consensus?
  • Is revision based on new evidence a weakness or a strength?
  • How certain do we need to be to act?
  • What's the quality of evidence supporting the current view?
12. False Analogy
Also known as: "Weak Analogy," "Faulty Comparison"
Weak Evidence Fallacy
üìñ Definition
Comparing two things that aren't actually comparable in the relevant ways. Using an analogy where the similarities are superficial but the differences are significant.
üîç How to Spot It:
  • "X is like Y, therefore what's true of Y is true of X"
  • Comparing complex systems to simple ones
  • "It's just like..." when the situations are actually quite different
  • Surface similarities used to prove deeper connections
  • Ignoring critical differences between the things compared
üìù Example:
"Weather forecasters can't predict next week's weather accurately, yet climate scientists claim to know what will happen in 50 years. It's laughable."
‚ùå Why It's Fallacious:

Weather (specific conditions at specific times) and climate (long-term patterns and averages) are fundamentally different things. The analogy ignores crucial differences. Just because two things share some features doesn't mean conclusions about one apply to the other.

‚ùì Questions to Ask:
  • Are these things actually comparable in the relevant ways?
  • What are the key differences being ignored?
  • Do the similarities run deep enough to support this conclusion?
  • Could a different analogy lead to the opposite conclusion?
13. Genetic Fallacy
Also known as: "Fallacy of Origins"
Relevance Fallacy
üìñ Definition
Judging something as good or bad based solely on where it comes from, who proposed it, or its history, rather than evaluating it on its current merits.
üîç How to Spot It:
  • "This idea came from X, so it must be wrong/right"
  • Dismissing arguments based on source rather than content
  • "Young people don't have enough experience..."
  • "That was developed during a different era..."
  • Judging an argument by demographics rather than merit
üìù Example:
"Climate activists are young people who haven't lived long enough to see weather patterns over decades. They lack perspective, so we can dismiss their concerns."
‚ùå Why It's Fallacious:

The origin or source of an idea doesn't determine its truth or value. Young people can present valid data and arguments. Old ideas can still be valuable. The merit of a claim should be evaluated based on evidence and reasoning, not on who proposes it or where it came from.

‚ùì Questions to Ask:
  • Is the criticism about the idea or its origin?
  • Would the argument be evaluated differently with a different source?
  • What evidence supports or contradicts the claim itself?
  • Are we judging the message or the messenger?

üéØ Final Tips for Identifying Fallacies

  • Separate the claim from the reasoning: Ask "What conclusion are they drawing?" and "What evidence supports it?"
  • Look for emotional language: Strong emotions can mask weak logic
  • Check for missing steps: Good arguments explain WHY the evidence supports the conclusion
  • Consider alternative explanations: If other explanations exist, the argument may be oversimplified
  • Watch for pressure tactics: "Everyone agrees," "You'd be foolish not to," etc. often signal weak arguments
  • Ask for specifics: Vague claims about "studies" or "experts" may hide weak evidence
